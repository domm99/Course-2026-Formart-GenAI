# Slides for the GenAI Course (Formart 2026)

## Context

The total duration is **31 hours**, divided into:

- 7 lessons of 4 hours
- 1 final lesson of 3 hours

The course should progressively move from basic concepts to practical use cases, with a strong final focus on how to use AI tools in everyday office work.

## Main Goals

By the end of the course, participants should be able to:

- Understand what data is and why it matters.
- Understand the basic ideas behind Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning.
- Understand what Large Language Models are and what they can and cannot do.
- Use AI tools for practical office tasks such as emails, reports, summaries, checklists, and document drafting.
- Write better prompts for conversational AI systems.
- Understand the main privacy risks when using online AI tools.
- Recognize the importance of GDPR and responsible data handling.
- Critically evaluate AI-generated outputs instead of trusting them blindly.

# Lesson 1 — Data, Artificial Intelligence, and Machine Learning

## Key Topics

- What data is.
- Structured data: tables, spreadsheets, databases.
- Unstructured data: emails, PDFs, text documents, images, audio.
- Personal, sensitive, business, and confidential data.
- Why data is the raw material of AI.
- Difference between data, information, and knowledge.
- Basic definition of Artificial Intelligence.
- Everyday examples of AI:
  - spam filters;
  - translation tools;
  - recommendation systems;
  - voice assistants;
  - autocomplete tools.
- Introduction to Machine Learning.
- Difference between traditional programming and learning from examples.
- Main types of tasks:
  - classification;
  - regression;
  - clustering;
  - generation;
  - reinforcement learning, explained only intuitively.

## Practical Angle

Use simple office-related examples, such as invoices, emails, customer requests, and reports, to show what kind of tasks AI can support.

# Lesson 2 — Privacy, GDPR, and Responsible Use of Data

## Key Topics

- Why data privacy matters.
- Difference between public, internal, confidential, personal, and sensitive data.
- Basic GDPR concepts:
  - personal data;
  - data processing;
  - data controller;
  - data processor;
  - data subject;
  - consent;
  - purpose limitation;
  - data minimization.
- Why AI tools create new privacy risks.
- What happens when users paste text into online AI tools.
- Difference between:
  - public chatbots;
  - business/enterprise AI accounts;
  - internally hosted models;
  - tools with contractual privacy guarantees.
- Differences between the European approach to privacy and other international approaches, especially the US.
- Practical checklist before using AI with real documents.

## Practical Angle

Participants should learn to identify data that should not be shared with online AI systems and practice anonymizing or simplifying information before using it in a prompt.

# Lesson 3 — How Machines Learn and Why Code Alone Is Not Enough

## Key Topics

- Traditional programming: explicit rules.
- Examples of rule-based systems:
  - calculating VAT;
  - checking mandatory fields;
  - sorting a table;
  - validating a form.
- Why explicit rules are not enough for complex problems.
- Examples of difficult rule-based tasks:
  - understanding the tone of an email;
  - summarizing a document;
  - recognizing complaints;
  - translating natural language;
  - identifying urgent requests.
- Learning from examples.
- What a dataset is.
- What training means, explained intuitively.
- What a model is.
- Prediction and generation.
- Common problems:
  - errors;
  - bias;
  - incomplete data;
  - wrong assumptions;
  - convincing but incorrect outputs.

## Practical Angle

Compare a manual rule-based approach with an AI-based approach for classifying simple emails or customer requests.

# Lesson 4 — NLP, LLMs, Embeddings, and Attention

## Key Topics

- Introduction to Natural Language Processing.
- Why human language is difficult for computers:
  - ambiguity;
  - synonyms;
  - context;
  - tone;
  - implicit meaning.
- What Large Language Models are.
- LLMs as systems trained on large amounts of text.
- How text generation works at a high level.
- Tokens: text split into smaller pieces.
- Why small prompt changes can lead to different outputs.
- Embeddings explained as “positions in a space of meaning”.
- Examples of semantic similarity:
  - contract, agreement, clause;
  - invoice, payment, deadline.
- Attention explained intuitively as focusing on the most relevant parts of the text.
- Main limits of LLMs:
  - they can invent information;
  - they can misunderstand context;
  - they do not automatically know what is true;
  - they should not be treated as authorities.

## Practical Angle

Show how different prompts produce different answers and discuss why the model reacts differently.

# Lesson 5 — Prompt Engineering

## Key Topics

- What a prompt is.
- Why vague prompts produce vague answers.
- Structure of a good prompt:
  - goal;
  - context;
  - role;
  - constraints;
  - output format;
  - examples;
  - quality criteria.
- Practical prompting techniques:
  - ask for clarification;
  - ask for multiple alternatives;
  - ask for tables;
  - ask for checklists;
  - specify tone and style;
  - specify length;
  - ask for assumptions and risks;
  - ask the model to revise or improve an answer.
- Common office prompts:
  - writing emails;
  - answering complaints;
  - summarizing meetings;
  - turning notes into minutes;
  - drafting reports;
  - rewriting in a professional tone;
  - simplifying technical language;
  - translating text.
- Common mistakes:
  - lack of context;
  - unclear objective;
  - no output format;
  - too many requests at once;
  - trusting the first answer;
  - sharing confidential data.

## Practical Angle

Hands-on lab where participants transform rough notes or informal emails into professional messages using iterative prompting.

# Lesson 6 — Online AI Tools and Office Automation

## Key Topics

- Difference between an LLM and an online AI product.
- Examples of AI tools:
  - ChatGPT;
  - Gemini;
  - Claude;
  - Copilot;
  - DeepSeek;
  - other AI assistants.
- The chat interface as a layer built on top of a model.
- Context, memory, history, file upload, and external tools.
- Why different tools may give different answers.
- What “memory” means in AI products.
- Practical office automation tasks:
  - drafting emails;
  - summarizing documents;
  - generating reports;
  - creating tables;
  - preparing presentations;
  - making checklists;
  - organizing tasks;
  - extracting action items from meetings.
- How to verify AI outputs:
  - facts;
  - numbers;
  - dates;
  - names;
  - legal references;
  - tone;
  - completeness;
  - privacy issues.

## Practical Angle

Simulate a typical office workflow: receive information, summarize it, identify priorities, draft a reply, and prepare a short report.

# Lesson 7 — Documents, Reports, Excel, CAD, and Technical Workflows

## Key Topics

- Using AI with long documents.
- Summarizing PDFs or reports.
- Extracting key points.
- Creating questions and answers from a document.
- Turning technical content into plain language.
- Writing reports:
  - title;
  - objective;
  - context;
  - analysis;
  - conclusions;
  - next steps.
- Adapting communication to different audiences:
  - colleague;
  - manager;
  - client;
  - technical operator.
- AI support for spreadsheets:
  - explaining formulas;
  - generating formulas;
  - cleaning data;
  - commenting on tables;
  - identifying anomalies;
  - describing charts.
- AI support for CAD and technical work:
  - writing technical descriptions;
  - preparing project documentation;
  - creating control checklists;
  - drafting specifications;
  - supporting operational procedures.
- Limits of AI in technical work:
  - AI does not replace technical responsibility;
  - outputs must always be verified;
  - formal correctness does not guarantee technical correctness.

## Practical Angle

Each participant builds a small personal prompt kit for recurring work tasks: email, report, summary, checklist, document analysis, and customer response.

---

# Lesson 8 — Limits, Reliability, Ethics, and Final Project

## Key Topics

- Do LLMs really reason?
- Difference between human reasoning and text generation.
- Why AI can sound confident even when it is wrong.
- Hallucinations.
- Overtrust and automation bias.
- Responsibility for AI-generated errors.
- Loss of critical thinking.
- Transparency issues.
- Dependence on external platforms.
- Golden rules for office use:
  - do not share confidential data without authorization;
  - never trust outputs blindly;
  - check facts, numbers, dates, names, and references;
  - use AI as support, not as final authority;
  - disclose AI use when required;
  - keep human responsibility in important decisions.

## Final Project

Participants complete a small practical task, such as:

- turning rough notes into meeting minutes;
- writing a professional email;
- summarizing an operational document;
- creating a checklist from a procedure;
- preparing a short report;
- building a reusable prompt for their job;
- identifying privacy risks in a sample text.
